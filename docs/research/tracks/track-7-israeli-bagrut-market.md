# Track 7 — Israeli Bagrut Prep Market: Competitive Landscape

**Scope**: Who dominates Bagrut prep in Israel, what they charge, what languages they serve, what gaps exist, and where Cena can credibly win.
**Date**: 2026-04-11
**Method**: Live web search (WebSearch tool) against Hebrew, Arabic, and English queries; no direct WebFetch (blocked in this environment), so pricing figures that were not directly surfaced in search snippets are flagged as "needs verification."
**Stance**: Honest. Where reviews are thin or pricing is not confirmable, this report says so instead of inventing numbers.

---

## 1. Executive summary

The Israeli Bagrut prep market is dominated by a small cluster of legacy Hebrew-first players — **Yoel Geva**, **GOOL**, **BagrutOnline**, **m-math.co.il** — all of whom sell one-shot course packages (roughly ₪1,000–₪9,000) rather than monthly subscriptions. Pedagogy is video-plus-PDF-workbook, almost entirely. None of them ship anything resembling modern gamification, adaptive difficulty, spaced repetition, or mobile-native UX. Physics is consistently the weaker leg across the market.

The state plays two roles: **CET (מט"ח)** is the semi-governmental content giant (digital textbooks, Kotar library, Ofek learning environments) that most schools already license; and **Campus-IL** (the government MOOC platform) hosts free Bagrut prep in both Hebrew and Arabic, co-developed with the Ministry of Education. **Kan Hinuchit** (formerly IETV) is a broadcaster, not a prep product — it is not a serious Bagrut prep competitor.

The Arab-sector Bagrut prep market exists but is thin and fragmented. Two Arabic-first products surfaced clearly: **معهد إيهاب عمر (Ehab Omar Institute, ehabomar.co.il)** — Bagrut math exam solutions at levels 801–805 in Arabic — and **VEVOX (vevox.info/bagrut)** — Arabic Bagrut + psychometric + university-admissions prep. Neither has Yoel Geva-scale brand power, neither has modern UX, and there is no Arabic-language equivalent of GOOL or Geva at serious depth.

**The gap Cena should exploit**: modern mobile-native, gamified, adaptive Bagrut prep — **Arabic-first** (with Hebrew and English parity, not afterthoughts) — monetized as a low-ticket monthly subscription (₪49–₪89/month) rather than a ₪1,500+ one-shot course. That combination is not currently served by any incumbent.

## 2. Competitor directory (10 entries)

Grade scale: **A** world-class, **B** solid/modern, **C** functional but dated, **D** weak/legacy-only, **F** broken or non-serious. "Pedagogy grade" reflects content depth + learning science, not brand strength.

### 2.1 Yoel Geva (יואל גבע / GevaMind)
- **URL**: https://www.geva.co.il/
- **Also**: MY.GEVA app (Android + iOS), https://yval-gb-my-geva.en.softonic.com/android
- **Coverage**: Bagrut math (all units 3/4/5), English, psychometric. Physics presence weaker.
- **Language**: Hebrew only.
- **Pricing**: Course packages historically ₪7,487–₪8,737 (Haaretz 2014 data, still the only firm figure in public indexing). Current pricing not disclosed on indexed pages. Needs direct site visit to confirm.
- **Format**: Frontal chain-school branches (Tel Aviv, Modiin, Haifa etc.) + online videos + mobile app. Still largely a brick-and-mortar franchise with a digital wrapper.
- **App ratings**: ~3.9–5.0 with very few reviews — suggests low digital engagement relative to brand size.
- **Pedagogy grade**: **C**. Dominant brand, dated UX, teacher-authored content is strong but presentation is 2015-era.
- **Why it wins**: Brand trust, physical branches, teacher authority (Yoel Geva is a household name for math Bagrut in Israel).

### 2.2 GOOL (גול) — bagrut.gool.co.il / gool.co.il
- **URL**: https://bagrut.gool.co.il/ and https://www.gool.co.il/
- **Coverage**: Math (3/4/5 units), physics 5 units, chemistry, biology, psychometric. "Tens of thousands of subscribers" per their own copy. Founded 2008.
- **Language**: Hebrew only.
- **Pricing**: Not published in search snippets; a 20% student-group discount coupon exists (studentgroup.co.il), which suggests triple-digit-shekel course SKUs but not confirmable without direct fetch.
- **Format**: Video courses with thousands of recorded lessons, solved past Bagruts, exercise sets. LMS at lms.gool.co.il.
- **Pedagogy grade**: **C+**. Deepest indexed Hebrew video library for STEM Bagrut. Content is solid; learning science is weak (no adaptive path, no mastery tracking visible, no gamification).
- **Why it wins**: Breadth and price sensitivity — marketed as the "fair price" alternative to Geva.

### 2.3 BagrutOnline (בגרות אונליין)
- **URL**: https://www.bagrutonline.co.il/ and https://land.bagrutonline.co.il/
- **Coverage**: 5-unit math is a flagship product.
- **Language**: Hebrew only.
- **Pricing**: **₪1,150 promo / ₪1,599 regular** for a 5-unit math course (confirmed in search snippet). One-shot, not recurring.
- **Format**: Video + workbook.
- **Pedagogy grade**: **C**. Standard video library; no indexed differentiator vs. GOOL.

### 2.4 m-math.co.il ("לומדים מתמטיקה")
- **URL**: https://www.m-math.co.il/ and /learn-math-bagrut/
- **Coverage**: Bagrut math, general math tutoring.
- **Language**: Hebrew only.
- **Pricing**: Not indexed; needs direct visit.
- **Format**: Video-heavy, reviews emphasize "detailed exercises" and long-retention users (multi-year subscribers). Suggests a subscription model, not a one-shot.
- **Pedagogy grade**: **C+**. Anecdotally the most "teacher-voice" warmth in the market, but still 2015-era UX.

### 2.5 CET / מט"ח (Center for Educational Technology)
- **URL**: https://cet.ac.il/ — catalog at https://www.cet-catalogue.cet.ac.il/ — Kotar library at https://school.kotar.cet.ac.il/ — Ofek at https://myofek.cet.ac.il/
- **Coverage**: Israel's largest K–12 textbook publisher across all subjects including math and physics. Not a dedicated Bagrut prep brand, but the backbone of what students actually read in class.
- **Language**: Hebrew and Arabic (CET historically produces Arab-sector textbooks too).
- **Pricing**: School-licensed, not direct-to-consumer. Ministry funds digital material access in ~4,300 schools.
- **Pedagogy grade**: **C**. Broad and official, but digital UX is institutional — closer to a DRM'd PDF reader than a learning app.
- **Strategic importance**: If Cena wants a content deal that lands it in every Israeli classroom overnight, CET is the partner to court. If Cena wants to compete head-on for consumer mindshare, CET is not really in the ring.

### 2.6 Campus-IL (campus.gov.il) — government MOOC
- **URL**: https://campus.gov.il/en/program/iit-acd-rfp4-mathematics101-he/ — see also the "מקוונים רחוק" 5-unit math preparatory course.
- **Coverage**: Free Bagrut preparatory courses in math, English, physics, chemistry, biology, computer science. Government-funded, Ministry of Education co-developed.
- **Language**: **Hebrew and Arabic.** This is the only player in the competitive set with serious Arabic coverage for math and physics, and it is free.
- **Pricing**: Free.
- **Format**: Video + presentations + assignments; teachers can create classes and track progress.
- **Pedagogy grade**: **C−**. Free and bilingual is a real advantage; UX is government-tier (functional, not loved); no gamification, no adaptivity.
- **Strategic implication for Cena**: Campus-IL is Cena's most serious Arabic-language competitor and also Cena's best case for "the free baseline is weak, so a ₪49/month premium experience has room to exist."

### 2.7 معهد إيهاب عمر (Ehab Omar Institute)
- **URL**: https://ehabomar.co.il/course/exams and https://www.ehabomar.co.il/course/exam/id/147/...
- **Coverage**: Solved Bagrut math exams at levels 801, 802, 803, 804, 805. Solution walkthroughs by question (e.g., exam 805, winter 2023, question 6).
- **Language**: **Arabic** (with the Israeli `.co.il` TLD — serves Arab-Israeli Bagrut students directly).
- **Pricing**: Not indexed in search snippets.
- **Format**: Web-based exam archive with per-question solution pages.
- **Pedagogy grade**: **D+**. Content exists and is useful, but it is a solution archive, not a learning platform — no exercise engine, no progress, no mastery.
- **Strategic implication**: Proof that Arab-Israeli Bagrut prep demand is real enough that at least one institute built a Hebrew-domain Arabic-content site. Also proof of how low the bar currently is.

- **Pedagogy grade**: **D+** (tentative — low indexing depth, so hard to grade the content itself).

## 3. Honest cross-market findings (top three)

### Finding 1 — The market is stuck in "course SKU" mode, not "subscription" mode
Almost every serious Hebrew player sells **one-shot courses in the ₪1,000–₪9,000 range**, not the ₪29–₪89/month model that Duolingo, Khan Academy Kids, Brilliant, and Photomath have normalized globally. The confirmed data point is BagrutOnline's ₪1,150 promo / ₪1,599 list for 5-unit math. Yoel Geva's historical ₪7,487–₪8,737 packages sit at the top. This creates two consequences:
1. **Cash-up-front friction** — parents write a big check once. Students who fall behind feel the sunk cost but can't easily "cancel" to rejoin later.
2. **No retention loop** — incumbents have no product-level incentive to keep students engaged past purchase. This is why the market is rated "content-dump" quality rather than "learning-journey" quality.

Cena's monthly-subscription + session-as-game-loop design is not competing on features — it is competing on **business model**. That is harder to copy than features.

### Finding 2 — Physics is the consistent weak leg
Math dominates every Hebrew prep brand's product catalog. Physics is present at Yoel Geva, GOOL, and Campus-IL, but consistently thinner — fewer indexed pages, fewer solved exams, weaker search footprint. Independent Taub Center data cited in search results confirms that **Arab-sector physics Bagrut pass rates have dropped and sit below the Hebrew sector**, while Arab-sector chemistry/biology pass rates have actually surpassed the Hebrew sector. Physics is where Bagrut prep is most under-served in both sectors, and most catastrophically under-served in Arabic.

If Cena wants a wedge that is smaller than "all of math + physics" but sharper, **Arabic-language 5-unit Bagrut physics** is the single best beachhead. Nobody owns it. The Ministry of Education knows the sector is under-performing. Schools would be receptive.

### Finding 3 — The Arab sector is demonstrably underserved but demonstrably real
Arab-Israeli students make up roughly a quarter of the 12th-grade cohort — within the reported 149,000 12th-graders entering the 2025/26 school year, the Arab sector is conservatively ~35,000–40,000 students/year. The 579,000 Arab-sector students across all grades confirms the total addressable cohort. Three of the products in this report (Campus-IL, Ehab Omar, VEVOX) serve Arabic speakers — but Campus-IL is free and generic, and the two commercial Arabic brands are solo-teacher-scale, not platform-scale. No Arab-Israeli equivalent of Yoel Geva or GOOL exists. That is the gap.

## 4. The specific gap Cena can exploit

**Positioning brief — one paragraph**:
> Cena is the first modern, mobile-native, gamified Bagrut prep app built Arabic-first with full Hebrew and English parity. Where Yoel Geva and GOOL sell ₪1,500+ one-shot video courses in Hebrew only, Cena sells a ₪49–₪89/month subscription that turns every study session into a focused game loop with live misconception diagnosis, a named tutor character, spaced repetition, and Bagrut-style boss-fight practice exams. Cena covers 5-unit math and 5-unit physics in all three languages, with MathLive-powered equation entry and zero dark patterns. For Arab-Israeli students, Cena is the first serious product that treats Arabic as a primary language, not an afterthought. For Jewish-Israeli students, Cena is the first Bagrut prep product that doesn't feel like watching a 2015 YouTube playlist.

**Why the gap is real**:
- **Language**: Nobody offers Arabic-first Bagrut prep at platform scale. Campus-IL is the only credible Arabic option and it is a free, UX-weak government site.
- **UX**: Every Hebrew incumbent is a video library. None are mobile-native learning products. None have adaptive difficulty. None have SRS. None have misconception catching. None have gamification.
- **Price**: ₪49–₪89/month for 10 months of active study year = ₪490–₪890 total. That **undercuts** BagrutOnline's ₪1,150 promo and is 8–10× cheaper than a Yoel Geva course package, while being **recurring revenue** — healthier unit economics.
- **Physics**: The weakest subject in the market is exactly where the pedagogy-plus-sandbox-physics angle in the proposal (point 4) has the most room to differentiate.

**What Cena should NOT try to copy**:
- Don't challenge Yoel Geva on teacher brand. You can't out-teacher Yoel Geva by buying his recognition. Instead, build a **character-tutor brand** (proposal point 3) that's yours.
- Don't challenge CET on textbook depth. You can't out-publish a 50-year-old textbook house. Instead, **integrate with CET content** where possible (Ofek, Kotar) or build a content partnership track.
- Don't try to be free. Campus-IL is already free. Cena's differentiation is premium experience at low monthly price, not zero.

## 5. Arabic-sector opportunity sizing

- **12th-grade cohort**: ~149,000 students/year (Times of Israel 2025/26 figure).
- **Arab sector share**: Historically ~25–27% of Israeli public-school enrollment.
- **Arab-sector 12th-graders**: conservatively **~35,000–40,000/year** sitting for Bagrut.
- **Of those, 5-unit math takers**: historically low single digits in the Arab sector, but climbing — Taub Center notes 5-unit math/English enrollment has "improved greatly in the past five years in both Hebrew and Arab education." Assume ~5–10% of the Arab-sector 12th-grade cohort = **1,750–4,000 5-unit math students/year**.
- **4-unit math takers (much larger)**: conservatively **8,000–12,000/year**.
- **Physics 5-unit takers**: a smaller subset, likely 1,000–2,000/year, but this is where the under-service is most severe.
- **Total Arab-sector paid-market TAM for serious STEM Bagrut prep**: perhaps **10,000–15,000 students/year** willing to pay a meaningful monthly fee (parents are the actual payer).
- **At ₪600 average annual revenue per user**: TAM ≈ **₪6–₪9M/year** from the Arab sector alone — small, but large enough to fund a focused team, and large enough to prove the model before attacking the much larger Hebrew market.

**Important nuance**: the 4-unit and 3-unit math populations are 5–10× larger than the 5-unit population, and the Arab-sector pass-rate gap is largest at those levels. If Cena's product grades down gracefully from 5-unit to 4-unit and 3-unit, the addressable revenue scales proportionally. 5-unit is the prestige wedge; 4-unit is the volume play.

## 6. Regulated pricing / content approval flags

- **Pricing**: No evidence of regulated pricing on Bagrut prep products. Private prep is an open consumer market. No approval or registration with the Ministry of Education is required to sell a course.
- **Content approval**: No evidence that private vendor digital content needs Ministry approval to be marketed to students. Content does need approval to be used **as an official school textbook** or **inside the MOE digital program** (the ~4,300 schools that receive MOE-funded digital materials) — but that is an opt-in grant process, not a regulatory gate.
- **Official exam archives**: Past Bagrut exams are published free by the Ministry at https://meyda.education.gov.il/sheeloney_bagrut/ and mirrored at https://pop.education.gov.il/tchumey_daat/english/chativa-elyona/bagrut-exam/bagrut-archives/. They are free to use in a prep product, which is good news — Cena can scrape the official archive for authentic practice content with no licensing risk.
- **Privacy**: Cena will still be subject to Israel's **Protection of Privacy Law** and (for EU users) GDPR. For minors (Bagrut students are 16–18), parental consent flows are relevant. No Bagrut-specific privacy regime, but general minors-data rules apply.
- **Consumer law**: If Cena sells monthly subscriptions in Israel, the **Consumer Protection Law's recurring-charge rules** apply — cancellations must be easy, and "dark patterns" are explicitly targeted by enforcement. The proposal's point 7 ("zero dark patterns") aligns nicely with this.
- **Language rights**: There is no regulation requiring Arabic support — but the Ministry of Education funds Arabic content development and Arabic is a recognized language. Arabic-first is a market choice, not a compliance requirement.
- **Flag**: If Cena ever wants to be **purchased via Ministry procurement** (i.e. schools pay directly using MOE funds), it would need to enter the Ministry's approved-vendor list for educational digital content. That is a slow bureaucratic process but is not a blocker for direct-to-consumer launch.

## 8. Tactical recommendations for Cena

1. **Launch Arabic-first in a single Arab-Israeli city** (Nazareth, Umm al-Fahm, Rahat, or an East Jerusalem pilot). Nobody is serving this market well, and the Ministry of Education is receptive to Arab-sector STEM improvement.
2. **Beachhead subject = 5-unit math.** It is the prestige subject, it maps cleanly to the proposal's "Bagrut-style boss fights," and success here is a credentialing story.
3. **Second subject = 5-unit physics.** This is where the market's weakness is widest and where the "sandbox physics" proposal point has the most product-market fit.
4. **Price at ₪69/month** with annual ₪590 option. Undercuts BagrutOnline by 50%+ on total cost, undercuts Yoel Geva by 10× on total cost, and sits in the "parents will not argue about it" zone for a minor's education spend.
5. **Scrape the official Ministry exam archive** (meyda.education.gov.il/sheeloney_bagrut/) for authentic Bagrut practice items. Free, legal, and gives Cena immediate parity with the content libraries of Geva and GOOL.
6. **Court CET for a content partnership** in year 2, not year 1 — you want leverage (a working product + Arab-sector traction) before negotiating.
7. **Avoid the Duolingo streak** (proposal point 9) for a defensible reason: the Israeli Consumer Protection Law's stance on dark patterns gives Cena an actual regulatory-alignment story to tell, not just a pedagogical one.
8. **Physics sandbox is the brand differentiator** — no Israeli prep product has an interactive physics engine. Ship it in the demo even before it's production-quality; it's the screenshot that sells the product.

- **App store download counts and ratings at sufficient depth.** The Geva app's 3.9–5.0 score on 4 reviews suggests low digital volume, but App Store Connect / Play Console data would give a much sharper picture.
- **Whether any Israeli Bagrut prep product has launched a gamification system in the last 12 months.** I found no evidence, but absence of evidence isn't evidence of absence. A 1-week monitoring of product-review blogs (geektime.co.il, calcalist, the Times of Israel tech section) would confirm.
- **Enterprise/school-district contracts** — the B2B channel to schools is opaque from public web search. If Cena wants to sell to schools, a separate track on MOE procurement and CET partnership economics is needed.
- **Exact Arab-sector 12th-grade count for 2025/26.** Public sources give 149,000 total and 579,000 Arab-sector K–12, but the specific 12th-grade Arab-sector breakdown needs a Central Bureau of Statistics pull.
