# Track 4 — Prodigy Math, DragonBox, Kahoot!

> Research sweep 2026-04-11 for Cena (EN/AR/HE math + physics, Bagrut/AP/SAT). Inline-returned by researcher agent; persisted here by coordinator.

## Executive summary

Three game-as-curriculum experiments, three different failure modes, three different lessons for Cena. Prodigy proves that wrapping math in RPG loot explodes engagement metrics while the actual math-per-hour collapses and pay-to-progress rots the classroom. DragonBox has genuine RCT evidence that its dragon-box mechanic teaches equation manipulation — and equally genuine evidence that the mechanic does not transfer to standard notation without adult scaffolding. Kahoot! has moderate evidence of anxiety *reduction* overall, but the speed-ranked leaderboard specifically is where anxiety concentrates, validating Cena's co-op-not-PvP stance on the speed dimension.

3. **Chan et al. (2023).** "Keep DRAGging ON: Is solving more problems in DragonBox 12+ associated with higher mathematical performance during the COVID-19 pandemic?" *British Journal of Educational Technology* 54. DOI 10.1111/bjet.13304.

## Prodigy Math — top 3 findings

**P1. The math-per-hour ratio is catastrophically low, by Prodigy's own numbers.** Fairplay's analysis, derived from figures in Prodigy's own commissioned research, says a student needs to answer ~888 math questions to gain ONE point on a standardized math test, which takes roughly 12 hours of focused play — and 30–40 hours for a typically-engaged child. CCFC documented up to 4× more advertisements than math exercises in a given session. Confidence: HIGH (Prodigy's own study, reinterpreted).

**P2. Evidence for Prodigy's efficacy is correlational, not causal.** The Johns Hopkins CRRE study is ESSA Tier-3 — no randomized control group. There are no published RCTs of Prodigy Math in peer-reviewed journals. Radically weaker than DragonBox's evidence base. Confidence: HIGH.

**P3. The "pay-to-progress" critique is documented at the regulatory level.** Feb 2021 FTC complaint from 22 child-advocacy groups, reinforced by Markey/Castor congressional letter: two-tier avatar system (members ride clouds, non-members walk in dirt — visible to classmates), in-game nagging, $59.88–$107.40/year, "free at school, pay at home" bait-and-switch. No FTC enforcement resulted, but documentation is airtight and reputational damage is permanent among curriculum leads. Confidence: HIGH.

## DragonBox — top 3 findings

**D1. The dragon-box mechanic genuinely teaches equation-solving procedures — RCT-confirmed at scale.** Decker-Woodrow 2023 is the gold-standard evidence: significant gains on algebraic understanding over business-as-usual. Confidence: HIGH.

**D2. Procedural gains do not cleanly transfer to standard algebraic notation without scaffolding.** Dolonen & Kluge: students who mastered the dragon-box could not reproduce the isolation procedure in standard notation. Chan 2023 JCAL: procedural gains at midtest *decreased* by posttest while conceptual knowledge was maintained. Independent critiques (Gieseking, Hammad) converge: DragonBox teaches *manipulation*, not *meaning*, unless an adult bridges the notation. Confidence: HIGH.

**D3. Effect size interacts strongly with prior knowledge.** Chan 2023 BJET dose-response: higher in-game progress predicted higher algebraic knowledge primarily for students with *higher* prior algebraic knowledge; end-of-year math achievement gains were *larger* for students with *lower* prior math achievement. Mechanism is non-uniform. For Bagrut/AP students already past introductory algebra, DragonBox-style mechanics may actually help more because they have the notation to transfer *to*. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH.

## Kahoot! — top 3 findings

**K1. Anxiety effect is net-positive but asymmetric.** Özdemir 2025 (43 studies) finds a moderate reduction in academic anxiety vs traditional review. BUT Wang & Tahir 2020 (93 studies) lists "stressful time-pressure," "not enough time to answer," "afraid of losing," and "hard to catch up" as the *most frequently reported* student complaints. Anxiety reduction concentrates in high-performers and those who would otherwise sit through boring review; it concentrates *against* the bottom half of the leaderboard. Confidence: HIGH.

**K2. Kahoot! is by design a low-depth review tool, not a learning tool.** Multiple reviews note Kahoot's reporting is shallow — who answered correctly, but not *why* students got it wrong or what misconception drove the error. Speed-scoring explicitly rewards pattern-matching and recognition, not reasoning. Confidence: HIGH.

**K3. Speed-ranking hurts reflective / slower processors regardless of ability.** Students who "process more thoughtfully often feel left behind" — the platform conflates speed with correctness. A student can be correct *and* publicly rank last in the same question. The 2025 Penn State / NC State *Instructional Science* study showed no meaningful learning-outcome differences between high- and low-competitive students on average — but the critique is about *who it hurts*, not the average. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH.

## Alignments with Cena proposal

- **Point 2 — Misconception-as-enemy**: DragonBox partially supports this. DragonBox *is* a misconception-as-enemy system — it weaponizes one specific misconception (that you can't move terms freely) and it works *for that one thing*. The critique — failure to transfer to standard notation — is Cena's warning: if the "enemy" is a custom metaphor the student defeats only in-metaphor, transfer fails. Fix: boss fights must be fought **in standard mathematical notation**, with the misconception explicitly labeled as the enemy. Keep the dragon-mechanic insight; drop the symbol hiding. **Delta: +1**.
- **Point 5 — Co-op not PvP**: Kahoot! research strongly supports this on the speed axis. Özdemir's anxiety-reduction finding is against a *traditional review* baseline, not against co-op. Co-op with no public speed-rank is strictly dominant. **Delta: +2**.
- **Point 7 — Zero streak guilt / variable-ratio rewards**: Prodigy's failure strongly validates this. Prodigy is the textbook cautionary tale of variable-ratio reward inflation. Cena's 888-questions-per-test-point figure is a specific, quotable anti-target. **Delta: +2**.
- **Point 1 — Session-as-game-loop with world map**: Prodigy IS a world-map game loop. It works at the engagement level and fails at the learning-density level. The lesson is not "don't build a world map"; it's "the world map cannot be the reward structure; it must be a navigation metaphor for curriculum topology." **Delta: 0** (unchanged — the difference is in framing, not feature).

## Mechanics to copy / avoid

**Copy from DragonBox:** (a) dose-response idea — in-game progress as valid learning measurement, not vanity metric; (b) surfacing standard notation from day one (unlike DragonBox); (c) mistake-friendly solving with undo and retry without penalty.

**Avoid from DragonBox:** (a) custom symbols replacing standard math notation; (b) assumption of automatic transfer; (c) pure procedural drilling without conceptual anchor.

**Copy from Kahoot!:** (a) instant per-question feedback (correct/incorrect with color + brief animation); (b) low-stakes review sessions; (c) the social feel of a shared classroom moment.

**Avoid from Kahoot!:** (a) public speed-based leaderboards; (b) shallow reporting that shows correctness but not misconception; (c) music + countdown + shame affective stack; (d) treating rapid recall as a proxy for understanding.

**Copy from Prodigy:** (a) RPG wrapper is legitimately motivating for K-8; (b) persistent identity (avatar) across sessions.

**Avoid from Prodigy:** (a) cosmetic paywalls of any kind ever; (b) two-class visible status (members vs non-members); (c) advertising to the child inside the product; (d) measuring engagement in minutes without measuring problems-per-minute; (e) correlational studies dressed up as efficacy claims.
